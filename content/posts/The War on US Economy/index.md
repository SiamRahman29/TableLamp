### The Asymmetrical War

If you have followed this war so far, you may have heard of the "Shahed Drone". The Shahed Drone has been Iran's standard attack option during this conflict. The best thing about this drone is not its range, nor its power, but how cheap it is. Reportedly, a Shahed Drone unit costs around $20,000-50,000. 

If you have been following the war, you may have heard of another piece of weaponry: the Patriot Missile defense system. It is a defense system from the United States that intercepts incoming missiles in mid air. From demonstrations and real-world deployment we have seen the system intercept missiles, but results are inconsistent. The interception often requires multiple missiles and sometimes they are totally unsuccessful in preventing a strike. Additionally, each Patriot missile (PAC-2) costs $3 to 4 million, and its newer generation (PAC-3) costs around $4-6 million.

Thus, the United States are having to burn through multiple millions of dollars of tax payer money to stop every 20-50 grand Iran throws at it. Iran has taken advantage of the asymmetry of its war with the US to make sure the battle is more than 15 times costlier for the US.

Iran has also utilized this asymmetry to take control of the strait of Hormuz. When multi-million dollars worth of oil tankers pass through the narrow strait of Hormuz, they have a maximum velocity of 20-30 kmh. They are easy targets for Iran's Shahed drones and Iran has already attacked ships that disobeyed their restrictions on the strait of Hormuz.

### Stagflation

In order to grow an economy, you have to invest. Due to the money being thrown at the war, the supply of investment is uncertain. The other thing you need to grow an economy are steady supply chains, and fuel is a part of every supply chain. Iran has already put strict restrictions on the strait of Hormuz in an attempt to stop oil exports, and they have already gained significant control of the narrow passage.

Now, the disturbance in the oil supply chain has a paradoxically coupled effect on the US. On one hand, oil prices are going up which is bound to make everything costlier. On the other, due to the global uncertainty, many countries are stockpiling oil which they can only buy with the US dollar. Therefore, the dollar demand is going up making the US dollar more valuable. So, local production for the United States could rise but with a stronger currency they are also able to save on imports.

Unfortunately, for the rest of the world's economy, fuel prices are going up and supply chains are vulnerable, making the world economy increasingly stagnant while inflation continues to rise. 

### Supply Chains Choked

The Strait of Hormuz is an integral part of several raw materials of key industries. One of them is LNG (Liquefied Natural Gas). Natural gas is a key material for fertilizer production. If a country cannot secure a steady production or supply of fertilizer, food shortages are imminent. 

### The Dollar Bubble

As I've mentioned earlier, with global uncertainty rising, the dollar value is also rising. At first, this sounds like good news for the United States. However, the increased prices for the dollar also means more reason for other global powers to establish an alternative to the petrodollar. With the Gulf countries weakening with Iran's attacks, they are under increased economic pressure. We know that the Gulf countries sell oil predominantly in exchange for the USD, but with the pressure from Iran if they bend and, hypothetically, start selling oil in Chinese Yuan, the US economy would be significantly weakened. The USD would lose value, and the United States would lose its power to impose economic sanctions, possibly resulting in the loss of the United States' status as the strongest global superpower.
