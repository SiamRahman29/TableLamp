At this moment, no matter where you are in the world, chances are you are feeling the effects of the war. The global economy continues to be unstable, while Iran continues to put pressure on the Strait of Hormuz. US President Donald Trump has set a deadline for Iran to open the Strait of Hormuz by April 6. Meanwhile, Israel continues to fight on the Lebanon front, and the US has carried out strikes on Iran's key energy infrastructure.

Where does this end? We have already discussed end scenarios that would be positive for the United States. Here are some endgames I believe are options for Iran and how much progress they have made toward those goals. But before that, we must keep in mind that "victory" for Iran would not be a surrender or the destruction of the US. It would be bringing the United States to the negotiation table, keeping their current regime in place, and securing positive economic deals.

### Unity

On the internet, there are reports of national unity in Iran. Most famously, there are reports of shop owners allowing customers to "take goods and pay after the war." Iran's state media have also released a video titled "One Revenge for All," where they pose themselves as a sort of avenger for all of the US' past atrocities, including the massacres of Native Americans and the Hiroshima-Nagasaki atomic bombings. These are signs that Iran is trying to win global opinion.

Iran is inferior to the United States when it comes to firepower and military strength. However, global opinion being on Iran's side makes it more difficult for the US and Israel to exercise excessive force. More importantly, having global opinion on its side makes Iran a more attractive ally.

Unfortunately, it is difficult for Iran to find allies among its neighbors due to the GCC's obedience to the United States. But there are limits. Moreover, powers in Europe and Asia have already expressed concerns regarding the United States' actions. Therefore, if Iran can achieve global unity that supports it beyond words and letters, it can force the United States to the negotiation table with decent leverage.

As I've mentioned already, Iran has taken steps to bring global opinion to its side, but achieving a level of unity that would support it when push comes to shove can be a luxury in a world where nations are more transactional and focused on their own economies. From the point of view of nations suffering from the pressure on the Strait of Hormuz, a swift resolution seems more beneficial than a resolution that is favorable for Iran. Furthermore, despite Iran having an Islamic identity, Muslim countries in the region are currently led by transactional governments with much to gain by supporting the United States. Expecting them to unite based on religious ideology while ignoring monetary gains is difficult.

### A Sweep Under the Rug

As I mentioned in the previous article in the series, Iran continues to put pressure on the dollar. Although the dollar is rising right now, it could lose value if the GCC makes the unlikely decision to start using alternatives to the petrodollar heavily. If Iran can put enough pressure on the GCC, and in turn on the US economy, then it can force the US government to withdraw. The people of the United States have already started putting pressure on Trump, and the president has already started blaming his lackeys for the war, which indicates his regret and could be interpreted as a sign that he may retreat. Thus, a retreat by the US government is the most plausible positive outcome for Iran.

If Trump retreats, he will probably not lose too much sleep over it, and he will divert attention to his next big "project" or scandal. He has followed this playbook before. However, before retreating, as a transactional and selfish person he is, he might try to gain personal benefits during the negotiations toward the end of the conflict.

Speaking of negotiations, Trump is scheduled to visit China in late April/early May. Right now, with pressure on the Strait of Hormuz, the Chinese economy is under pressure as well because it depends on the GCC for energy imports. However, it could also be said that the United States is hurting itself more than it is hurting China.
