On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel attacked Iran. The US dubbed this attack "Operation: Epic Fury" and the Israel dubbed it "Operation: Roaring Lion". They targeted nuclear facilities, military intelligence sites, the ministry of defense, the ministry of intelligence, and one girls' elementary school. The death toll was reported to be 550, and 165 were reported to be children, though some news outlets cite higher numbers. Among the dead were also Iran's Supremem Leader Ayatollah Khomeinei, who was killed along with his family members, including his grandchildren, when an airstrike leveled his residence in Tehran.

Since then, there have been strikes and counter-strikes involving Iran, Israel, Lebanon, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Bahrain and Qatar. Many people all over the world are interpreting this conflict as an omen of world war 3. I have analyzed the ongoing conflict and the recent history of the main actors of the war to map out the possible endgames for all parties involved.

### The United States' Possible Endgames

#### Scenario 1: All Out Attack?

At first glance, one might write this conflict off as a victory to the US given their enormous military capabilities and their lack of remorse or ethics demonstrated during the Israeli genocide. In a way, this prediction is possible. The United States possess the ballistic and nuclear arsenal to level all of Iran a few times over. But in today's world of open trade and economic collaboration between the nations, there is a limit to the violence a nation can inflict, even for the US. Cross that limit, the mutual collaboration turns into mutual destruction. So, this is not really a winning scenario for the United States, and, consequently, not very likely. 

#### Scenario 2: A Regime Change

The US foreign policy has a pattern it has followed since the second world war. Conduct military invasion of foreign lands under the colors of freedom and liberty to topple the government that is not obedient to you and replace it with one that is. They have followed this playbook in the middle east and most recently in Afghanistan.

Prior to the attacks of February 28, large scale protests against the current theocratic government were ongoing. Thus, with the attacks Iran has entered one of its most politically volatile state in its history. The US will want to appoint a "Shah" or monarch as they did after 1953's coup in Iran. Their candidate of choice is Reza Pahlavi, son of Iran's last shah, who has been in exile since the Islamic Revolution in Iran in 1979. Reza Pahlavi has already spoken publicly about his support for the US and its allies, including Israel. He has also garnered support from a part of the Iranian population and its diaspora. Reza Pahlavi has tried to create an image as a liberal modernizer, making himself the candidate of choice for the US.

By installing a shah in Iran, the US will have a leader in their control running one of most critical states in the middle east. If the monarchy is re-established, it will be the end of the inter-state conflict, at least temporarily, with the US having its interests secured.

The US has been working towards this goal for a few years now, evidently with their support for student protests against the current government.

#### Scenario 3: Civil War in Iran

Iran is an ethnically diverse state, with 12 distinct ethnic groups scattered across its borders. The Persian majority, at roughly 61%, is concentrated in the country's interior. Along the periphery live significant populations of Iranian Azerbaijanis, Turkic peoples, and Arabs with deep cultural ties to Azerbaijan, Turkmenistan, and the Arab states to Iran's south. There are other minorities living in the border regions as well.

![Ethnic map of Iran](/Ethnic_Map_of_Iran.png)

The United States could deform this diversity into division by squeezing Iran's resources. The country has been battling severe water shortages and drought for three years. Rainfall dropped by 45% in 2025 compared to the previous year. Lake Urmia, once Iran's primary freshwater source, has largely dried up due to years of unsustainable extraction. Iran's response to this crisis has been slow and largely ineffective, leaving the country on the edge of collapse since late 2025. It had been investing in desalination plants, a solution adopted by nearly all its Gulf neighbors, but those plans have now been thrown into jeopardy by the recent attacks, and the water crisis is accelerating rapidly.

As resources dwindle, ethnic tensions tend to rise. The US could pour fuel on those flames by arming regional militias. Should the discontent reach a tipping point, civil war could force Iran to abandon its external war effort and potentially trigger a regime change in the process.

### The United States' Progress on its Goals

For both Scenario 2 and Scenario 3, the US entered the conflict from a position of advantage: protests were already underway, and food and water were already scarce. The attacks so far have pushed conditions closer to Scenario 3. However, Iran's geography has forced US strikes to concentrate on the plains rather than the mountains, as ballistic missiles are poorly suited to mountainous terrain. This has placed Tehran at the center of the bombardment.

Paradoxically, Tehran is where Iran's liberal and progressive population is most concentrated, the very people most open to regime change and least inclined toward a fight to the finish. By targeting the capital so heavily, the US risks alienating the Iranians most likely to support them, and in doing so, may be inadvertently strengthening national unity rather than fracturing it.

Although the United States has made progress to achieve these goals, it has not been without sever costs to itself.
